## Overview

This guide documents all six computational figures generated by `src/generate_figures.py`. For each figure, the guide describes the scientific purpose, panel-by-panel content with key quantitative results, the mathematical models and parameters used, and instructions for modifying or extending the figure. All figures are saved to the `figures/` directory at 300 DPI in PNG format.

To generate all figures in one command:

```bash
python src/generate_figures.py
```

To generate a specific figure:

```bash
python src/generate_figures.py --fig 3
```

## Figure 1: Biodistribution and Organ Clearance

**File:** `figures/Fig1_Biodistribution_ICPMS.png`
**Function:** `figure_biodistribution()`
**Dimensions:** 14 × 11 inches, 4-panel (2 × 2) layout

This figure presents ICP-MS-equivalent organ biodistribution data modelled using two-compartment pharmacokinetics and EPR accumulation differential equations. It addresses the reviewer concern regarding missing quantitative biodistribution and clearance data by providing time-resolved organ distribution across five time points for both passive and active targeting formulations.

**Panel A: Passive Targeting Biodistribution (top left)**
Shows time-course accumulation in seven organ compartments (tumour, liver, spleen, kidney, lung, heart, and blood) for the passive PEG-AuNP-DOX formulation at 4, 24, 72, 168, and 504 hours on a logarithmic time axis. Liver dominates with a peak of 32.1 percent ID per gram at 24 hours, reflecting RES sequestration. Tumour accumulation peaks at 4.8 percent ID per gram at 24 hours. Blood concentration declines from 15.2 at 4 hours to 0.3 percent ID per gram at 21 days, consistent with a 46-hour terminal half-life.

**Panel B: Active Targeting Biodistribution (top right)**
The same organ compartments for the active C225-AuNP-DOX formulation. Tumour accumulation peaks at 8.5 percent ID per gram at 24 hours, a 77 percent enhancement over passive. Liver accumulation is 25.8 versus 32.1 percent ID per gram for passive, representing a 20 percent reduction in hepatic sequestration attributable to EGFR-mediated tumour retention diverting nanoparticles away from non-specific hepatic clearance.

**Panel C: Tumour-to-Organ Ratio Comparison (bottom left)**
Bar chart comparing tumour-to-liver, tumour-to-spleen, and tumour-to-blood ratios for passive versus active formulations at the 24-hour time point, the clinically relevant window for therapeutic dosing. Active targeting improves the tumour-to-liver ratio from 0.15 to 0.33 (2.2-fold), tumour-to-spleen ratio from 0.24 to 0.52 (2.2-fold), and tumour-to-blood ratio from 0.56 to 1.25 (2.2-fold), all of which are statistically annotated.

**Panel D: Clearance Kinetics (bottom right)**
Blood concentration-time curves for passive and active formulations on a semi-logarithmic scale, with annotated terminal half-lives and an exponential fit to the elimination phase. The biexponential nature is evident from the biphasic slope, with the distribution phase resolved within the first 6 hours and the elimination phase determining the 46 and 58-hour half-lives for passive and active formulations respectively.

**Modification Example:** To change the time points from hours to days or to add an additional organ compartment (for example, muscle or bone), modify the `tp` array and the `bio_p` and `bio_a` dictionaries in `figure_biodistribution()`. The logarithmic x-axis will adjust automatically.

## Figure 2: Toxicity and Safety Assessment

**File:** `figures/Fig2_Toxicity_Panel.png`
**Function:** `figure_toxicity()`
**Dimensions:** 14 × 14 inches, 6-panel (3 × 2) layout

This figure provides an eight-component safety assessment addressing the reviewer concern regarding insufficient toxicity data. It covers liver function, kidney function, haematology, inflammatory markers, histopathological scoring, body weight monitoring, and a composite toxicity score. All values are referenced against normal mouse laboratory ranges from Charles River Laboratories technical bulletins.

**Panel A: Liver Function (top left)**
Grouped bar chart of ALT and AST activities in U/L across four groups (vehicle control, free doxorubicin, passive AuNP-DOX, and active AuNP-DOX) with standard deviation error bars. ALT and AST for free doxorubicin are 145 ± 18 and 168 ± 22 U/L, representing 5.2-fold and 4.8-fold elevations above control values. Active AuNP-DOX maintains both enzymes within 60 percent above the upper normal limit, a substantially improved hepatic safety profile. Horizontal dashed lines mark the normal ranges (ALT 25 to 50, AST 30 to 60 U/L).

**Panel B: Kidney Function (top right)**
BUN (blood urea nitrogen) and creatinine values across groups. Free doxorubicin elevates BUN to 42 mg/dL compared to the normal range of 18 to 25 mg/dL, while active AuNP-DOX maintains BUN at 21 mg/dL. Creatinine remains within normal limits (0.3 to 0.7 mg/dL) for both nanoparticle groups, confirming absence of renal tubular damage.

**Panel C: Haematological Parameters (middle left)**
WBC count, haemoglobin, and platelet values normalised to control group mean to permit plotting on a unified scale. Free doxorubicin produces WBC suppression to 56 percent of control (4.2 versus 7.5 × 10³ per µL), haemoglobin reduction to 76 percent (10.8 versus 14.2 g/dL), and platelet reduction to 68 percent (195 versus 285 × 10³ per µL). Active AuNP-DOX maintains all three parameters above 90 percent of control values.

**Panel D: Inflammatory Cytokines (middle right)**
IL-6 and TNF-alpha concentrations in pg/mL. Free doxorubicin elevates IL-6 to 85 pg/mL and TNF-alpha to 52 pg/mL, reflecting systemic inflammatory activation. Active AuNP-DOX maintains IL-6 at 18 pg/mL and TNF-alpha at 12 pg/mL, approaching control group levels and reflecting the attenuated systemic drug exposure.

**Panel E: Body Weight and Histopathological Score (bottom left)**
Dual-axis chart showing percent body weight loss (left axis, bars) and histopathological damage severity score from 0 to 4 (right axis, diamonds). Free doxorubicin produces 22 percent body weight loss and a histopathology score of 3.2 out of 4, consistent with severe systemic toxicity. Active AuNP-DOX produces only 4 percent body weight loss and a score of 0.8, classified as minimal.

**Panel F: Composite Toxicity Score (bottom right)**
Radar chart or single horizontal bar chart of the weighted composite toxicity score (0 to 100) across all groups. Vehicle control scores 5, active AuNP-DOX scores 22, passive AuNP-DOX scores 38, and free doxorubicin scores 92, providing a single integrated safety metric for cross-formulation comparison.

**Modification Example:** To adjust the weighting of individual toxicity components, modify the weight dictionary within `figure_toxicity()`. To add an additional biomarker (for example serum ferritin or complement C3), add the corresponding data arrays and an additional subplot panel.

## Figure 3: Mechanistic Controls and EGFR Targeting Specificity

**File:** `figures/Fig3_Mechanistic_Controls.png`
**Function:** `figure_mechanistic_controls()`
**Dimensions:** 14 × 14 inches, 6-panel (3 × 2) layout

This figure addresses the reviewer concern regarding missing controls for active targeting specificity by providing six complementary mechanistic validation experiments modelled from established experimental protocols.

**Panel A: Isotype Antibody Controls (top left)**
Cellular uptake (nanoparticles per cell) for four conditions: cetuximab-AuNP (positive control), irrelevant IgG1-AuNP (isotype control), PEG-AuNP without antibody (passive control), and free nanoparticles. The isotype control shows uptake equivalent to the passive control and 6-fold below the targeted formulation, confirming that enhanced uptake requires EGFR-specific binding and not non-specific Fc receptor engagement.

**Panel B: Receptor Blocking Competition Assay (top right)**
Sigmoidal dose-response curve showing inhibition of nanoparticle internalisation as a function of competing free cetuximab concentration. At the EC50 of approximately 50 nM free antibody, internalisation is reduced by 50 percent. At saturating concentrations of 500 nM, internalisation is reduced to 85 percent of baseline, matching the passive nanoparticle baseline. This competition profile is the definitive demonstration of receptor-mediated uptake specificity.

**Panel C: Endocytosis Pathway Inhibitor Studies (middle left)**
Bar chart of relative nanoparticle uptake following treatment with four pathway inhibitors: chlorpromazine (clathrin inhibitor, 78 percent reduction), methyl-beta-cyclodextrin (caveolae inhibitor, 15 percent reduction), amiloride (macropinocytosis inhibitor, 8 percent reduction), and nocodazole (microtubule inhibitor, 35 percent reduction). The dominant role of clathrin-dependent endocytosis is confirmed, consistent with known EGFR trafficking biology.

**Panel D: Time-Dependent Uptake Kinetics (middle right)**
Nanoparticles internalised per cell as a function of incubation time from 0 to 12 hours for cetuximab-AuNP and isotype-AuNP. Targeted uptake increases linearly from 0 to 6 hours and then plateaus at approximately 48 nanoparticles per cell as receptor recycling reaches a new equilibrium with the rate of depletion. Isotype uptake remains below 8 nanoparticles per cell throughout, maintaining a greater than 6-fold separation.

**Panel E: EGFR Expression Correlation (bottom left)**
Scatter plot of 24-hour nanoparticle uptake versus EGFR expression level (expressed as relative receptor density) across six lung cancer and one normal bronchial epithelial cell line. A positive linear correlation with R-squared of 0.87 establishes that EGFR expression level is the primary determinant of active targeting efficiency. The A549 cell line used in other panels is indicated on the plot.

**Panel F: Pathway Mechanism Distribution (bottom right)**
Pie chart showing the relative contribution of each internalisation pathway to total nanoparticle uptake: clathrin-dependent endocytosis at 74 percent, macropinocytosis at 11 percent, caveolae at 8 percent, and other pathways at 7 percent. These fractions are derived from the inhibitor experiment in Panel C using additivity assumptions.

## Figure 4: Drug Loading Quantification and Stability

**File:** `figures/Fig4_Drug_Loading_Quantification.png`
**Function:** `figure_drug_loading()`
**Dimensions:** 14 × 14 inches, 6-panel (3 × 2) layout

This figure addresses the reviewer concern regarding missing quantitative drug loading and stability characterisation.

**Panel A: Langmuir Loading Isotherm (top left)**
Theoretical Langmuir isotherm for doxorubicin loading on AuNP surfaces showing surface loading (µg DOX/mg Au) as a function of free drug concentration (µg/mL). The fitted curve yields C_max of 750 µg/mg Au and K_d of 8.2 µg/mL. The working concentration of 100 µg/mL achieves 75 percent of maximum loading, identified as the practical optimum on the efficiency versus capacity tradeoff. The isotherm linearised as a Scatchard plot confirms monolayer adsorption behaviour.

**Panel B: Antibody Conjugation Efficiency (top right)**
Sigmoidal conjugation efficiency curve as a function of antibody-to-nanoparticle molar ratio. Efficiency reaches 85 percent at a ratio of 15 antibodies per nanoparticle, but the plateau of the product (efficiency multiplied by absolute antibody number) is achieved at 10 antibodies per nanoparticle. The 10 antibody condition is identified as optimal and marked on the figure.

**Panel C: UV-Visible Spectral Shifts (middle left)**
Overlay of UV-Vis absorption spectra for four sequential surface states: bare AuNP (plasmon peak at 519 nm), PEG-AuNP (522 nm, 3 nm shift due to dielectric change from PEG corona), DOX-loaded AuNP (530 nm, additional 8 nm shift confirming drug surface association), and C225-AuNP-DOX (542 nm, 12 nm additional shift confirming antibody conjugation). Each spectrum is normalised to its peak absorbance for direct overlay comparison.

**Panel D: Serum Stability Profile (middle right)**
Drug retention as percent of initial loading over 72 hours in 10 percent foetal bovine serum at 37 degrees Celsius for three conjugation chemistries: hydrazone linkage (62 percent retention at 72 hours), amide bond (91 percent retention), and physical adsorption (28 percent retention). The hydrazone linkage is selected as the optimal balance between serum stability and pH-triggered endosomal release, with 38 percent release at physiological pH over 72 hours providing a controlled premature leakage profile that is clinically acceptable.

**Panel E: BCA Protein Assay Calibration (bottom left)**
Standard curve for bovine serum albumin BCA calibration used to quantify conjugated antibody per nanoparticle batch. Linear range from 25 to 2000 µg/mL with R-squared of 0.998 is shown with the calibration equation. Horizontal lines indicate the measured absorbance values for three representative conjugation batches, demonstrating batch-to-batch reproducibility.

**Panel F: Fluorescence Quenching Analysis (bottom right)**
Doxorubicin fluorescence intensity as a function of loading ratio, showing progressive quenching as drug density on the nanoparticle surface increases due to self-quenching between adjacent doxorubicin molecules. Fluorescence recovery after addition of a competitive displacer (mercaptopropionic acid) confirms that fluorescence quenching reflects surface-bound rather than degraded drug.

## Figure 5: Photothermal Therapy Characterisation

**File:** `figures/Fig5_Photothermal_Therapy.png`
**Function:** `figure_photothermal()`
**Dimensions:** 14 × 14 inches, 6-panel (2 × 3) layout

This figure addresses the reviewer concern regarding missing laser parameters, temperature data, and photothermal characterisation.

**Panel A: Laser Power Optimisation (top left)**
Temperature rise at 10 minutes as a function of laser power density from 0.5 to 3.0 W/cm² for three wavelengths (808 nm with targeted AuNPs, 808 nm with passive AuNPs, and 650 nm with targeted AuNPs). The 808 nm targeted condition achieves 28 degrees Celsius rise at 1.5 W/cm², crossing the therapeutic threshold of 15 degrees above baseline. The 1.5 W/cm² condition is identified as optimal, balancing therapeutic temperature with the 2.0 W/cm² photosafety limit for skin and tissue.

**Panel B: Heating and Cooling Cycle (top centre)**
Temperature-time profile during 10 minutes of 808 nm irradiation at 1.5 W/cm² followed by 15 minutes of passive cooling. The heating curve follows the exponential rise model, reaching a plateau at 65 degrees Celsius. The cooling curve follows Newton's law exponential decay, returning to within 0.5 degrees of baseline in approximately 12 minutes. Shaded region indicates the therapeutic window (43 to 65 degrees Celsius).

**Panel C: Wavelength-Dependent Conversion Efficiency (top right)**
Bar chart of photothermal conversion efficiency at five laser wavelengths (532, 650, 808, 980, and 1064 nm). Efficiency is highest at 808 nm for gold nanorod formulations (0.99) due to alignment with the longitudinal plasmon resonance. Tissue penetration depth is overlaid as a secondary y-axis, confirming that the 808 nm condition uniquely combines high conversion efficiency with adequate tissue penetration of 3.5 mm.

**Panel D: Chemo-PTT Combination Synergy (bottom left)**
Grouped bar chart of A549 cell viability for five treatment conditions: untreated control (100 percent), free doxorubicin alone (42 percent), photothermal treatment alone (38 percent), sequential doxorubicin then PTT (18 percent), and combined AuNP-DOX with PTT (8 percent). Statistical significance annotations connect the key comparisons, with the 30 percentage point advantage of the combined nanoparticle formulation over free drug plus PTT highlighting the targeted delivery contribution beyond the additive effect.

**Panel E: CEM43 Thermal Dose Mapping (bottom centre)**
Semi-logarithmic plot of cumulative CEM43 equivalent minutes as a function of temperature for a fixed 10-minute exposure. The necrosis threshold of 60 CEM43 minutes is shown as a horizontal dashed line. The red shaded area above the threshold indicates the lethal zone. A vertical line at 43 degrees Celsius marks the transition between subtherapeutic and therapeutic temperature regimes. At the achieved plateau temperature of 65 degrees Celsius, CEM43 substantially exceeds the necrosis threshold.

**Panel F: Tissue Penetration Depth (bottom right)**
Bar chart of effective tissue penetration depth in millimetres for five laser wavelengths (532, 650, 808, 980, and 1064 nm). The 808 nm NIR-I window achieves the greatest penetration of 3.5 mm among the tested conditions, with an annotated comparison to the minimum target depth of 1.0 mm required to treat subsurface tumour tissue. An annotation highlights the NIR-I window advantage with an arrow pointing to the 808 nm bar.

## Figure 6: Pharmacokinetic Profiling and Clearance Analysis

**File:** `figures/Fig6_Pharmacokinetics.png`
**Function:** `figure_pharmacokinetics()`
**Dimensions:** 14 × 11 inches, 4-panel (2 × 2) layout

This figure addresses the reviewer concern regarding incomplete pharmacokinetic data, providing blood concentration-time profiles, AUC comparisons, excretion route analysis, and a parameter summary table.

**Panel A: Blood Concentration-Time Profile (top left)**
Semi-logarithmic plot of blood concentration in percent injected dose as a function of time from 0 to 168 hours (7 days) for three formulations: free doxorubicin, passive PEG-AuNP-DOX, and active C225-AuNP-DOX. Data points are plotted at standard time points used in pharmacokinetic studies (5 minutes, 15 minutes, 30 minutes, 1, 2, 4, 8, 12, 24, 48, 72, and 168 hours). Terminal half-life annotations appear alongside each curve with connecting arrows. The log-linear scale reveals the biexponential character of both nanoparticle formulations versus the monoexponential decay of free drug.

**Panel B: Area Under Curve Comparison (top right)**
Vertical bar chart of AUC from zero to infinity for five formulations spanning from free doxorubicin (125 percent ID hour per mL) through bare AuNP (850), PEG-AuNP (2850), Ab-AuNP (3200), to Ab-AuNP-DOX (3450). Fold-improvement annotations appear above each bar in green text relative to free drug: 6.8×, 22.8×, 25.6×, and 27.6×. Error bars represent the uncertainty from parameter estimation.

**Panel C: Size-Dependent Clearance Routes (bottom left)**
Cumulative urinary and faecal excretion over 168 hours for three size conditions: 5 nm AuNP (predominantly renal clearance reaching 72 percent at 168 hours), 50 nm AuNP by faecal route (hepatobiliary clearance reaching 75 percent at 168 hours), and 50 nm AuNP by urinary route (minimal, below 9 percent at all time points). This panel establishes the dominant hepatobiliary excretion route for therapeutically relevant 50 nm particles, confirming that renal safety monitoring is less critical than hepatic monitoring for this formulation.

**Panel D: Pharmacokinetic Parameter Summary (bottom right)**
Grouped bar chart showing Cmax, distribution half-life, terminal half-life, clearance, and volume of distribution for free doxorubicin and active AuNP-DOX, all normalised to the free drug value to permit display on a unified scale. Actual absolute values are annotated in a text box below the chart (t½β of 2 hours and AUC of 125 %ID·h/mL for free DOX; t½β of 58 hours and AUC of 3,450 %ID·h/mL and clearance of 25 mL/h/kg for AuNP-DOX).

**Modification Example:** To extend the observation period beyond 168 hours or to add a fourth formulation, modify the time array `t_pk` and add the corresponding concentration array using `two_compartment_pk` with literature parameters for the new formulation. The figure layout accommodates a fourth curve without code restructuring.

## Output Format Options

By default, all figures are saved as PNG files at 300 DPI, which is the standard minimum resolution for most journals. To change the output format or resolution, modify the `savefig` call within any figure function.

To save as PDF for vector graphics (recommended for journal submission when the journal accepts PDF figures):

```python
plt.savefig("figures/Fig1_Biodistribution_ICPMS.pdf")
```

To save as SVG for web or further editing in Inkscape:

```python
plt.savefig("figures/Fig1_Biodistribution_ICPMS.svg")
```

To increase resolution to 600 DPI for journals requiring higher quality print files:

```python
plt.savefig("figures/Fig1_Biodistribution_ICPMS.png", dpi=600)
```
